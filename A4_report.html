Dataset: The dataset I decided to use was Abortion Data by U.S. State from the Guttmacher Institute. While familiarizing myself with this dataset, I immediately noticed how while the dataset was constructed as a comparison between 2017 and 2020, it included variables like “No. of abortions, by state of residence, 2020” for 2020 but no corresponding variable for 2017. Instead the dataset include “No. of abortions among women aged 18-19, by state of residence, 2017”, however these two variables can’t ethically be compared to each other. In addition, the dataset consisted of many non discrete values with the cells instead filled with “unavailable”, “n/a” or “<1,000”.

Background: At the time in 1973, the U.S. Supreme Court’s ruling in Roe v. Wade legally recognized the right for an abortion, however this didn’t guarantee the availability of proper health care providers for abortion. In fact, in response to the Supreme Court decision, several states moved forward with strict regulations on abortion clinics including the necessary width of corridors and size of procedure rooms in the clinic.

Proposition: In the United States, there are not enough abortion clinics.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • While understanding the data, I experimented with various relationship, including abortion rate among women aged 15-44 by state and the percentage change in the number of abortion clinics. Another angle I explored was the correlation between change in abortion rates and clinic numbers from 2017 to 2020. My goal was to plot these two independent variables against each other in order to emphasize how for each state with an increase/decrease of abortions there was a corresponding increase/decrease of abortion clinics. While the variables statistically had a relatively strong correlation, the line plot didn’t effectively communicate this. While a data analyst would likely notice the correlations at the peaks, to a general audience the lines weren’t perfectly in sync and thus didn’t communicate that the number of clinics was adequate.
  • Instead I decided to shift my focus to comparing the number of residents seeking out-of-state care to the total population of each state. This aimed to demonstrate that in-state clinics are generally sufficient, by showing that the number of residents going out of state for abortion care was small in comparison to the total number of residents.
  • Initially, I started my representation with a multidimensional bar chart which had two bars for each state - one for the total population and the other for the # of residents going out of state for abortion care. However this proved to be too complex and cluttered, especially when including all 50 states.
  • I ultimately decided to use a 100% stacked bar chart, illustrated the proportion of residents seeking out of state care again the total population, for a cleaner and more impactful visual. When plotting this data, an interesting note is that each column consists of [Total Residents][Residents Seeking Out of State Care]. One of the reasons I decided against plotting the columns like [Total Residents - Residents Seeking Out of State of Care][Residents Seeking Out of State Care] was to help convince the audience into that residents seeking out of state care was in fact a smaller fraction of the population - statistically however the graph is accurate. These were some of the design choices I made for which type of plot to choose and also how to represent the data.
  • The color scheme was strategically chosen to highlight the data's implications: a bright green for the total population and a light red for those seeking out-of-state care. This choice was meant to visually emphasize that the majority of residents rely on in-state care, using the contrast between red and green to denote the negative connotation of seeking care elsewhere. Visually, the plot is dominated by the bright green color, which influences the viewer into believing that a vast majority of residents are satisfied with in state abortion care. In addition another important consideration was whether the # of Residents Seeking out of state abortion care could be at the bottom of each column or from the top. I ultimately decided to keep this measure at the top because our attention is drawn to the core of the graph (closer to the x-axis) and we only see trickles of red peaking at the top.
  • Overall, I would rate this plot on a scale of deceptiveness as a -1. My design accurately represents the data in its entirety, including all 50 states and the actual values were not influenced. However, the scaling of how my data is represented in proportion to each other diminishes the visual representation of how many residents are seeking out of state care. For instance in Mississippi, technically 99% of residents are seeking out-of-state abortion care - this number of awfully high and represents that there aren’t enough in-state clinics. However, with my representation of Total Number of Residents to # of Residents seeking out of state care, the column for Mississippi is actually only around 50% red. The reason that in my proportion the value comes out to be 50% is because let’s say the total number of residents is 100 and 99% percent of the residents seek out-of-state care, thus 99 residents. Plotting 100 and 99 against each other, the bar for red/green both come out to be around 50%. The less deceptive proportion visualization would have been to represent the bar visually at 99%, with 1 to 99 (residents who didn’t seek out-of-state care vs. residents who seek out-of-state care). In addition the colors I used were in an effort to guide the viewer’s focus, potentially downplaying the significance of those seeking out-of-state care, to help negate the statement that there aren’t enough abortion clinics. I believe design choices like colors shouldn’t be considered unethical or deceptive.

FOR the Proposition

Objective of Design #2: The main goal of Design #2 as to support the statement that there aren’t enough abortion clinics.

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I shifted to a multidimensional bar chart, contrasting the number of counties without abortion care in 2014 vs. 2020. This visualization emphasizes how the majority of states have nearly all counties lacking in abortion care, with little improvement over 6 years. My choice of strong, contrasting colors - foundation blue and red - I believe enhanced the clarity and avoided clutter (previously seen when trying the previous design’s color palette on this bar chart).
  • In addition, I decided to represent these 2 independent variables as multidimensional bar chart rather than 100% stacked bar chart, like used in Design #1, because I wanted to highlight how the percentage changed between 2014 and 2020. The change between 2014 and 2020 is better seen with the two bars next to each other for comparison.
  • I opted to exclude the District of Columbia because it had 0 countries - while Connecticut also behaved as an outlier with 0% of its counties without abortion care, it was included in the plot due to its eight counties.
  • On a scale for how deceptive my design #2 is, I would argue 1 because I believe its a clear representation of data, including only the non-comparable care of District of Columbia. With my design decision and careful selection of neutral colors, I aimed to present the facts unambiguously. The height of the bars themselves demonstrate the extent to which there exists a widespread lack of abortion care facilities across the states.
  • Another design choice which I made was to make the background light gray - I believe that this improves the overall readability, focus and aesthetic appeal of the graph. In addition with light graph, my goal was to enhance the contrast between the bars making the data stand out more and also I feel that a softer, gray background is easier on the eyes in comparison to a bright white background. I also added a bright red line at both the 0 and 100% mark - this was to highlight the bars for each state. My goal was to also emphasize how a majority of the bars were very close to the red 100% line. This line also stretches across the whole x-axis, thus allowing the audience to compare the height of the bar to the percentage very easily. I don’t feel that any of these design choices were particularly unethical, but instead were meant to enhance the message of the data already demonstrates.

Final Reflection

Overall, through this assignment I have developed two graphs from the dataset which seem to be proving opposing perspectives on whether there are enough abortion clinics. With one graph, using different proportions between the given data, I was able to show that there are in fact enough abortion clinics to the general audience. However in addition to design this graph, I had to go through several iterations - starting with different data relationships and types of plots (i.e. line plots, pie chart).

If we had to draw bounds in order to distinguish between “acceptable” persuasive choices and “misleading” ones, I would consider playing with the data (excluding, scaling) as more misleading while design choices like (positioning, color) as more persuasive choices. I believe it’s important to include accurate representations of proportions and scales, while using visual elements to distort data’s actual proportions (ex. using graph elements to misrepresent the data’s significance) I would consider to be unethical. In addition, I believe designing plots in a way which aim to inform the decision-making for audience is completely ethical while intentionally designing the plot in a way to manipulate the audience’s opinion and obscure the truth is unethical.

Although I didn’t not change the data values given to us for design #1, I still believe that ethically it was not correct because of the proportions I intentionally used. In an effort to deceive the audience, I compared the total population with respect to total residents who sought out-of-state care. So that when the general audience looks at the plot, since the length of the red bar (representing residents seeking out-of-state care) is shorter, they are influenced into believing that the number of abortion clinics are sufficient. I believe that this tactic was in fact unethical because we are purposefully representing the data in a way which manipulates the audience. However, I believe that changing colors in order to emphasize one side as negative or positive is not necessarily unethical. I don’t see this as unethical as you are not changing proportions or scaling of the data but instead enhancing readability and accurate comparison of the data through colors. Another factor which I believe is unethical is cherry-piking data (excluding certain data points) which would alter the interpretation of the data. This was why although both District of Columbia and Connecticut had 0% of counties without abortion care, I only excluded District of Columbia since it was technically not valid. I don’t see changing which side the red bar is on (at the top or closer to x-axis) is unethical. Although design techniques are used to enhance the presentation of the data, the data is not being misinterpreted nor are differences being exaggerated.
